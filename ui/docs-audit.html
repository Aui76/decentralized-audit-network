How an audit works

One contract, from posted to settled. Every step is an on-chain action — nothing is decided by hand.

Posted → Assigned → Checked → Challenge period → Settled

Posted

A protocol posts a contract and a bounty, and names the check that applies. The check is fixed at this moment and can't be changed later.

Assigned

An auditor from the pool is offered the contract. The protocol accepts them — or stays silent, and it accepts automatically. No one races for work; assignment is structural.

Checked

The auditor runs the named tool against the contract and gives one answer: pass or fail. Nothing in between.

Challenge period

A pass doesn't pay right away. A fixed waiting period opens — time for anyone to prove a bug the auditor missed. This adversarial slot is permanent by design, not a delay.

Settled

If the challenge period closes with no bug proven, the audit is confirmed: the auditor is paid and the record is final.

When a bug is found

A fail at the check, or a bug claimed during the challenge period, opens a dispute. An independent auditor re-checks the original contract — re-running the claimed tool on the same deployed code.

If they reproduce the bug, the original audit is marked exploited and the finder is paid from reserves — no new tokens are minted for a failure. If they can't reproduce it, the claim is rejected, the original audit stands, and the finder loses their stake.

Settlement is always the result reproduced on the original contract — never a patch, and never some other procedure finishing on your behalf.

Permanent and reproducible

Settled and exploited are final. The whole history is on-chain, and anyone can recompute any verdict from the live code.
