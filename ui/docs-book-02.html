The idea

Chapter 1 named the gap: immutable code, and a market that sells trust in a name instead of a check strangers can repeat.

The idea is the opposite shape. A protocol where independent security researchers audit smart contracts under published rules — not under a client’s private engagement letter. The output is not a PDF to believe. It is a binary verdict — pass or fail — recorded on-chain, backed by artifacts anyone can re-run: same tool, same pinned code, same declared spec, same answer.

Payment and credibility follow that record. An auditor is not paid merely for shipping a report. Incentives tie to whether the line they certified stays true after strangers had time to probe it during the challenge window. Findings that survive independent re-run move value; findings that do not, do not.

That is the whole pivot: replace the opinion with a result.

The loop in plain language

At the highest level — details in later chapters — one audit looks like this:

  1. A protocol locks reward and declares what “pass” means for this contract.
  2. An eligible auditor is assigned under published rules.
  3. The auditor re-runs that declaration and posts pass or fail with proof.
  4. On a pass, a challenge window opens: anyone can file a proof-bearing claim if the check missed something reproducible.
  5. If the window closes clean, the auditor earns a confirmation reward; if a challenge reproduces, the record and payouts update through the challenge procedure (break path) under the same rules.

Every step is designed so a stranger can verify what happened from public data — not from trusting who wrote the slide deck.

The Audit Cell is the on-chain attempt to make this idea real: contracts that do not care who you are, only whether the digests match and the window rules were followed.
